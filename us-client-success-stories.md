    <article class="case-study">
      <div class="case-study__header">
        <div>
          <span class="card-label">Restaurant Expansion</span>
          <h3>$150,000 working capital loan</h3>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__meta">
          <span>Texas</span>
          <span>Hospitality</span>
          <span>Speed-driven</span>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="case-study__details">
        <div>
          <strong>Situation</strong>
          <p>A Texas restaurant group needed fast capital to secure a fourth location and start buildout before losing the site.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Constraint</strong>
          <p>The opportunity would weaken quickly if the capital moved through a slower bank-style process.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Structure</strong>
          <p>Working capital structure with a 1.25 factor and a 9-month term, tied to card-based revenue patterns.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Why it fit</strong>
          <p>The business needed speed more than perfect long-term pricing, and the repayment could be supported by the expected revenue lift.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__detail case-study__detail--full">
          <strong>Outcome</strong>
          <p>The location opened within six weeks and annual revenue increased materially after launch.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
    </article>
    <article class="case-study">
      <div class="case-study__header">
        <div>
          <span class="card-label">Manufacturing Upgrade</span>
          <h3>$500,000 term loan</h3>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__meta">
          <span>Michigan</span>
          <span>Manufacturing</span>
          <span>Asset-backed</span>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="case-study__details">
        <div>
          <strong>Situation</strong>
          <p>A Michigan fabricator needed a new CNC machine without accepting a blanket lien across all assets.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Constraint</strong>
          <p>The business wanted growth capital, but not a structure that would overreach on collateral and restrict future flexibility.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Structure</strong>
          <p>Fixed-rate term loan secured primarily by the equipment, with early payoff flexibility.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Why it fit</strong>
          <p>The asset could support the debt more naturally than a broad working-capital structure, and monthly payment predictability mattered.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__detail case-study__detail--full">
          <strong>Outcome</strong>
          <p>Production time dropped, new contracts were won, and the company repaid the facility ahead of schedule.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
    </article>
    <article class="case-study">
      <div class="case-study__header">
        <div>
          <span class="card-label">Contractor Cash Gap</span>
          <h3>$750,000 bridge loan</h3>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__meta">
          <span>California</span>
          <span>Contracting</span>
          <span>Timing bridge</span>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="case-study__details">
        <div>
          <strong>Situation</strong>
          <p>A California contractor had a municipal project but needed liquidity before net-60 invoice payments came in.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Constraint</strong>
          <p>Waiting on receivables would have slowed the project and damaged the company’s ability to keep labor and materials moving.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Structure</strong>
          <p>Six-month bridge loan with interest-only payments to cover materials and subcontractor costs.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Why it fit</strong>
          <p>The gap was temporary, the receivables path was visible, and the bridge preserved execution speed without forcing the wrong permanent product too early.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__detail case-study__detail--full">
          <strong>Outcome</strong>
          <p>The project stayed on schedule, the borrower repaid after receivables landed, and the company won follow-on work.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
    </article>
    <article class="case-study">
      <div class="case-study__header">
        <div>
          <span class="card-label">Chicago Property</span>
          <h3>CRE refinance with cash-out</h3>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__meta">
          <span>Illinois</span>
          <span>Mixed-use CRE</span>
          <span>Refinance</span>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="case-study__details">
        <div>
          <strong>Situation</strong>
          <p>A mixed-use property owner needed to replace an expensive loan and free up capital for improvements.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Constraint</strong>
          <p>The existing debt was too expensive to keep carrying, but the next structure still needed to support renovation plans.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Structure</strong>
          <p>Long-term refinance at a lower fixed rate with amortization support and a cash-out component.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
          <strong>Why it fit</strong>
          <p>The property had enough stability for a refinance path, and the lower monthly burden mattered more than a fast short-term bridge.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="case-study__detail case-study__detail--full">
          <strong>Outcome</strong>
          <p>Monthly payments fell, renovation capital was unlocked, and property performance improved.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
    </article>
