    <h1>Use asset-backed debt when the purchase should pay for itself over time.</h1>
    <p>Equipment financing is often cleaner than using working capital for machinery, vehicles, medical devices, restaurant equipment, or production tools. The structure should match the useful life of the asset and the cash flow it helps create.</p>

<section class="section">
  <p class="eyebrow">What lenders usually care about</p>
  <h2>Equipment debt gets stronger when the asset and the business support each other.</h2>
  <div class="grid grid--3">
    <article class="service-card">
      <span class="card-label">Asset quality</span>
      <p>The equipment type, age, resale value, and whether it is standard or specialized all affect how comfortable the lender can be.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="service-card">
      <span class="card-label">Business support</span>
      <p>The file is stronger when the business can show revenue stability or a clear operating reason why the asset improves output, margin, or capacity.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="service-card">
      <span class="card-label">Structure match</span>
      <p>Good equipment financing uses a term that fits the asset life and does not force the business to carry unnecessary payment pressure upfront.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="section split">
  <div class="panel">
    <p class="eyebrow">When equipment debt is cleaner than a term loan</p>
    <ul class="plain-list">
      <li>The purchase is clearly tied to a specific vehicle, machine, or device</li>
      <li>The asset itself improves lender comfort and reduces the need for a broader collateral grab</li>
      <li>The business wants to preserve working capital for operations instead of folding the purchase into a larger general-purpose loan</li>
    </ul>
    <p>If the capital need is broader than the asset, a term loan can still be the better structure.</p>
