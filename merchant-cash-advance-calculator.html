Best use of this tool

Use it to compare tradeoffs, not to justify the first approval.

  • Lower factor rate can still create worse pressure if the term is compressed.
  • Daily remittance often matters more than the headline offer size.
  • The cleaner working capital offer is the one the business can absorb without creating the next problem.

How to interpret the result

Cheaper is not always easier

A lower-cost offer can still create worse weekly or daily strain if the payback window is too compressed for the business rhythm.

Monthly drag is the fastest truth test

If the business cannot carry the implied monthly load without stressing payroll, inventory, or vendor timing, the deal is weak even if it closes fast.

Use the outputs as a screen

The better offer still depends on remittance mechanics, renewal pressure, documentation requirements, and whether this type of working capital is the right product at all.

Advisor lens

When I would actually like this kind of working capital, and when I would slow the file down.

When working capital can be the right tool

Short-term timing gaps, inventory turns, bridge payroll, or urgent receivables mismatches can justify this kind of working capital if the business can clearly carry the remittance rhythm.

When I start pushing back

If the monthly drag is already crowding payroll, taxes, or vendor terms, a fast approval is not enough. That is when LOC, factoring, or a smaller structure may be cleaner.

What makes the file stronger

Recent bank statements, a coherent use of proceeds, and a realistic payback story matter more than trying to sound polished. Good files usually look consistent, not dramatic.
